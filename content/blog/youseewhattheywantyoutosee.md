A few years ago, an extremely popular Chinese reality TV show had its appearance in Prague. The cast of this show included some famous S-tier Chinese celebrities of huge fanbases. Take it as if renowned figures of the Western world like Leonardo Di Caprio, Taylor Swift, Will Smith, or Angelina Jolie etcetera had a convocation in your city—people obviously went bananas.

The show's concept was simple. Each episode had a theme that determined the aspect of gamification, ranging from often bizarre activities as Mouth Rock-Paper-Scissors, Speed Quiz Game (Charades Style), Water Piggyback, or the iconic Acupuncture Stone Skipping. At the start of each episode, the cast received their clothing with name labels on the back, divided into two teams, and by completing activities they received points for their group. In the last stage of each episode, the teams battled each other with the goal of tearing off as many name labels as possible of the opposite team. The final results weren't as important since the audience was mostly hooked by the shiny personalities, quirky comments, or jokes that happened throughout the episode.

Back then, I had the opportunity of being the cast's personal Czech translator or be part of the filming audience, but I ended up with the latter. From what I remember, I was too shy and intimidated to step out and let the better opportunity just slip through my hands. But still it was quite an experience anyhow.

Firstly, special requirements were announced beforehand. In order to adhere to the cultural equality of this particular aired episode, we were asked to bring domestic peers and companions along with us. In a way, it made sense to me since China and Czechia were striving to seal their political relationship. Therefore, my then classmate and I arrived at the Eden Arena (now Sinobo Stadium) expecting nothing. Neither of us knew why exactly we joined the event—we barely knew the show—but as kids, we thought it would make a perfect story to brag about. "*Yo guys, I talked to Emma Watson of China yesterday, what you had been up to?*"

Right at the entrance, we were bewildered. The organizers separated us into two lines and assigned different colors of T-shirts, red for my line and blue for my friend's one. Once I reached the main venue, it hit me. For the next four hours, I will be standing next to a bunch of strangers I've never met, expressing my utter excitement for the show by waving flags, and singing chants I never thought I would while my friend is probably in the same situation but at the completely opposite side of the stadium—all across from me.

By the time the celebrities arrived, my legs were sore already and I felt so out of place, but what kept my hopes high was their promise of getting us a KFC meal after the shooting day. So I stood there and cheered when I had to, half of the time watching some "hilarious" games through the little space I was left by other fans. In this particular episode, it was a battle of nations, and the colors represented two fandoms.
As I mentioned above, the Chinese cast was usually divided into teams but this time they all competed against the Czech group of celebrities.

A special message for my Czech folks reading this:
>Byla to fraška. (It was a farce.)

The director of the show purposefully designed the episode to put an emphasis on cultural exchange, but my big point is how skewed the reality of TV shows is.

Normally, it didn't matter who would win but this time it was a huge deal-breaker for mainstream media. If I'm not mistaken many of the episode's sports events were retaken since the Czech won in almost every discipline. In between the sessions, the director personally told guests to diminish their dominance and pretend their inferiority. That's what happens when you invite an opposing team full of athletes, duh. But funnily enough even after that nothing much changed... 

The biggest problem was that the activities were made for Czechs—you can't expect to win a beer-chugging contest in Prague—having to inadvertently end the episode in favor of Czechia.

### Conclusion
A horrendous Jimmy Fallon’s episode triggered this memory. How much of what we see actually happened or is real? Audiences are controlled to laugh at particular moments, the jokes of comedians are scripted to seconds, clap on three, say what they want to hear, or even “evil editing.” It is a common practice for an editor to recompile specific footage or an audio file in a way so it distorts the original meaning to produce new misleading information, attention-worthy clickbait.

Although, it is counterproductive to think in this way since leisure is meant for relaxation but we often don’t see in between the lines of scripts which have an immense influence on our enjoyment of media. All of this leads me to think that in this world we’re all just living in someone else’s planned reality.🌎
